Publications

B. Guduri, M. Cybulsky, G.R. Pickrell, and R.C. Batra. Development of Adaptive Process Control to Achieve Consistent Particles’ States in Atmospheric Plasma Spray Process, Journal of Thermal Spray Technology (in submission).

Abstract
Development of Adaptive Process Control to Achieve Consistent Particles’ States in Atmospheric Plasma Spray Process
The desired consistency in quality of atmospheric plasma spray (APS) coatings is crucial in many applications, which is difficult to achieve due to the complexity of the APS process. It has been established that the consistency in mean particles’ states such as mean axial velocity and mean temperature before impact on the substrate is a key aspect of achieving consistent coating quality. In this paper, an attempt is made to develop an adaptive process control to achieve consistency in mean particles’ states. The strategy is first to predict the optimal inputs for desired mean particles’ states and minimize the error between the measured and the desired mean particles’ states by adjusting significant inputs when a parameter drift occurs. The analyses are carried out using LAVA-P, numerical software simulates the APS process. First, screening analysis using one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) approach by Morris is carried out to investigate the significance of seven process parameters: current, argon and hydrogen flow rates, injector location, powder feed rate, and stand-off distance on the mean particles’ states. This analysis suggests that the significant process parameters for the mean particles’ states are argon and hydrogen flow rates, current, and injector location from the jet axis. Using linear regression analysis, quadratic response functions for the mean particles’ states are identified in terms of these significant process parameters. These functions are used to construct an objective function and optimization problem is solved by genetic algorithm for finding optimal values of argon flow rate, hydrogen flow rate and current for a given location of injector. It is found that these optimal values achieve mean particles’ states within 5% their prescribed desired ones. These optimal values are used to estimate initial gains for proposed adaptive process control and its performance is tested on LAVA-P to attenuate the influence of artificially induced disturbances such as average injection velocity of particles and arc voltage on the mean particles’ states. The mean particles’ states are reached desired values within the settling time of 50 ms by mitigating the effect of disturbances and the smooth variation in the inputs and the mean particles’ states are obtained.

Numerical modeling of plasma spray process

Previous Next

Over the last two decades, atmospheric plasma spray (APS) process became one of the most efficient and reliable techniques to produce a wide variety of coatings such as low porosity, thermal barrier, wear and corrosive resistant coatings in different applications [1]. A large variety of powder materials can be used to spray since these materials can efficiently melt due to the high temperature of the plasma jet. The APS process is a common technique to produce ceramic coatings, and is capable of producing functionally graded material coatings [2]. Because of cost efficiency and versatility, numerous industries have adopted this process to produce high-performance coatings [3]. Due to the demand on high volume manufacturing of these coatings, reproducibility and repeatability of the coating quality are prime importance [4].

The APS process is complex process includes plasma generation, plasma and in-flight particle interaction and deposit formation on a substrate. Complex interactions between plasma and particles significantly [4], [5] vary coating properties that affect the process repeatability [6]. Due to large velocity and temperature gradients in a plasma jet, even small changes in process parameters can significantly alter mean particles’ states (mean temperature and axial velocity at the instant of striking the substrate), and thus the quality of the coatings [7]. Even with the operating parameters apparently set constant during the process, the particles’ states before impacting the substrate can change over time due to noise variables such as nozzle wear, injector wear, pulsing of powder due to leaks, worn parts in the powder feeder and powder dampness. These fluctuations and noise variables could limit the applicability of an APS process to high precision applications. The motivation, then, for the development of adaptive process control for achieving desired consistency in the mean particles’ states before impacting the substrate is to improve repeatability and reproducibility of coating quality and simultaneously decreasing manufacturing costs.

Due to lack of experimental set-up, we carried out the analyses using fortran code, LAVA-P, which wad developed at the Idaho National Engineering and Environmental Laboratory (INEEL) to simulate APS process. The numerical simulations are inexpensive as compared to manufacturing and material cost and enable us to explore a large variable space. The LAVA-P software simulates the Ar – H2 plasma jet along with particle injection, acceleration, melting, re-solidification and evaporation. The following assumptions are made to simulate the APS process:

  1. The Ar – H2 plasma jet is an axisymmetric, unsteady, compressible, Newtonian, turbulent, and chemically reacting multi-component fluid mixture with temperature-dependent thermodynamic and transport properties [30].
  2. Plasma is in local thermodynamic equilibrium (LTE).
  3. Chemical reactions among different species including ionization, dissociation, and recombination are considered.
  4. The plasma generation in the nozzle is not simulated.
  5. The arc voltage fluctuations in the plasma nozzle are not considered, and the mean voltage is assumed to be steady.
  6. Effects of the electromagnetic field in the plasma are neglected.
  7. The standard turbulence is considered and turbulence modulation is neglected.
  8. The effect of powder injector geometry is not considered.
  9. The particles are assumed as spherical.
  10. The effects of gravity and buoyancy on the plasma jet and particle trajectories are neglected. These forces are negligible compared to viscous drag force [31], [32].
  11. The effect of internal convection within the molten particles is neglected.
  12. No mutual interactions among particles occur.
  13. The surface of particles is not oxidized.
  14. The effect of carrier gas flow rate on plasma jet is neglected. It was emphasized that the carrier gas flow rate of 5 slm (Standard Liter per Minute) has a negligible effect on the plasma characteristics [5].
  15. Zirconia powder is considered.
  16. For particle heating, 1D heat conduction model with melting, solidification, and evaporation are considered.
  17. The randomness of the particle injection is considered.

The detailed description of the governing equations with initial and boundary conditions of the plasma generation are described in [30]. The Ar-H2 plasma jet profile computed using LAVA-P has been validated by Ramshaw et al. for the Metco 9MB plasma gun in [30]. Here, we compare computed results with the experimental findings of Williamson et al. for the Miller SG-100 plasma gun [33] when pure Ar is used to generate the plasma. The L2-norm of differences between the computed and the experimental results are less than 29.2%. The simulated values of the mean axial velocity and the mean temperature are compared with experimental results of Smith et al. [34] and the computed results agree well with the corresponding experimental ones with the maximum difference between the two being 7%.

Screening of process parameters

Previous Next

The screening analysis for plasma spray process is done by ranking the strength of the impact of process parameters (inputs) on the mean particles’ states (outputs). The outcome of this screening analysis is a list of significant process parameters, which are used for identifying response functions for the mean particles’ states.

Here, we focus on seven process parameters, which are current, argon flow rate, hydrogen flow rate, powder feed rate, spraying distance, injector location along the jet (y-) axis and along (z-) axis. We use the global one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) screening analysis proposed by Morris [1]. [1] M. D. Morris, “Factorial sampling plans for preliminary computational experiments,” Technometrics, vol. 33, no. 2, pp. 161–174, 1991. This analysis provides a ranking of the effects of process parameters on the mean particles states which are a) negligible b) linear and additive, or c) nonlinear and/or involved in interactions with other process parameters. This is done by calculating so-called elementary effects (EE) of the randomly chosen value of each process parameter at a time on the mean particles states.

Based on this approach, the argon flow rate, the current, the y location of the injector and the hydrogen flow rate are considered as significant process parameters for developing response functions.

Process parameter optimization

Previous Next

Three steps involved in finding the optimal values of process parameters for desired mean particles’ states are screening, identification of response functions, and optimization. The screening analysis is done by ranking the strength of the impact of process parameters on the mean particles’ states. The outcome of this screening analysis is a list of significant process parameters, which are used for identifying response functions for the mean particles’ states. We used a complete polynomial of degree 2 for mean particles’ states in terms of these significant process parameters. The statistical significance of main and interaction effects (i.e. variable terms in polynomial) are determined using the analysis of variance (ANOVA). This analysis suggests which terms in the polynomial are insignificant to reduce the complexity of polynomial; and corresponding coefficients are estimated using linear regression analysis. Finally, the resulting response functions for the mean particles’ states are used to construct an objection function. Then the optimization problem is solved for finding optimal values of significant process parameters using genetic algorithm.

The percentage error between simulated and desired mean particles’ states is less than 4 % except for one case. For the desired values of mean axial velocity of 100 m/s and mean temperature of 3100 K, the solutions from these two algorithms show a maximum 4.2 % error with simulated ones. This is due to lack of an optimum solution in assumed parameter space. These approaches are valid only if the values of process parameters are in the range of values used for developing response functions. The predicted process parameters from GA optimization will be used to estimate initial parameters for a control algorithm. Note that identified response functions are valid when other process parameters are not changing.

System identification in plasma spray process

Previous Next

In an atmospheric plasma spray process, several physical phenomena such as chemical reactions among species; turbulence in plasma jet; particle heating, melting, and vaporization; and particle acceleration are involved. Due to the complexity of the process, finding an exact differential equation between inputs and outputs is one of the challenging issues for developing an efficient control system.

One can develop approximate mathematical models between the inputs and the outputs based on the known information, which are classified into black box, grey box or white box model. The black model (E.g. ANN) implies the model is estimated based on the input-output data without any prior information about the structure of a physical plant. The grey box model implies that the model is developed from the structure of plant dynamics and its parameters are estimated using identification techniques from input-output data. In the white box model, the structure and its parameters are already known from physics. We focus on developing a grey model for mean particles’ states since particles' axial velocity and temperature are governed by particle acceleration and 1D heat conduction model, respectively. The structure of this model is obtained by linearization of nonlinear dynamics of particle states around an equilibrium point. This linear model would possess the information about steady-state and transient behavior of the mean particles’ states when the inputs have changed from an equilibrium point. The coefficients of the linear model can be estimated by the system identification approach using the given input-output data.

To estimate these coefficients, we used the following steps:

  1. Designed numerical experiments randomly from Latin hypercube sampling using sufficiently rich input signals to excite the plant dynamics around an operating point.
  2. Simulated mean particles states from LAVA-P software for chosen inputs’ variations
  3. Estimated the coefficients of linearized models using input-output data from simulations based on the prediction error minimization (PEM) framework.
  4. Validated the estimated models using input-output data which was not used for the estimation.

The estimation of coefficients of linearized models is carried out using the MATLAB toolbox ‘ident’ with function ‘ssest’. The responses of estimated models of the mean axial velocity are shown in good agreement with the given output data with an average fit percentage of 85 % for training and 82 % for validation. Whereas for the mean temperature, the responses of the estimated models satisfactorily agree with 72 % for training and 66 % for validation. The estimated parameters for the mean axial velocity and the mean temperature of 50 samples are distributed closer to each other with a few outliers.

Course project: Nanomechanics

Previous Next
Dynamics of water confinement between two graphene sheets

Souvick Chatterjee, Balachandar Guduri, Nejib Masghouni

At the nanoscale, structural and dynamical properties are quite different from the macroscopic phenomena. Fluid flow between parallel plates, a common problem in bulk-liquid, is still not well-understood in the atomic scale. Characteristics like diffusion, solvation of biomolecules are important phenomena relying on the problem studied herewith. In this work, we studied in detail the effect of two parallel graphene sheets confining a solvation box within. Based on potential energy comparison, hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity of the system has been analyzed. Also, the effect of different axial and perpendicular initial velocities of one/two graphene sheet(s) on characteristics of the system have been discussed.

The results obtained in this work opens up a whole area of field which deserves attention. Interesting phenomena are observed through MD simulations which is obviously not uncommon provided the scale we are dealing with. Both physical explanation and MD results should converge in the long run providing a thorough understanding of such nano-scale phenomena.

Such a problem of water confinement between hydrophobic surfaces is in itself an interesting phenomenon. To add to that, the application of velocity in different directions, physically perturbing the well-known Couette flow configuration can explain properties like slip, molecular diffusion, and interfacial characteristics. Also, of considerable interest is finding the thermal conductivity of such a configuration and potential usage of the same in nano-scale heat transfer problems. Not only graphene sheets but analysis of general Couette flow can also be generalized as carbon sheet-water interaction problem and can be used to solve problems like Couette flow in cylindrical coordinates, an example of which is water confinement between multi-walled carbon nanotube, with an applied initial velocity to one of the nanotubes.
